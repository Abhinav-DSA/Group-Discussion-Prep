## Introduction

India’s linguistic tapestry—over **19,500 dialects** and **22 scheduled languages**—embodies its cultural richness and linguistic diversity. In 1968, the **Three-Language Formula** was introduced under the National Policy on Education, aimed at bridging regional divides. Aimed at linking the Hindi-speaking north with the diverse south and east, it faced fierce opposition, notably from Tamil Nadu, over perceived Hindi imposition.

---

## Objectives and Features of the Formula

Proposed by the **Kothari Commission (1964-66)**, the Three-Language Formula sought to unify India’s linguistic diversity through education. The formula included **three languages**—regional language or mother tongue, official language, and a modern Indian or European language. This triadic model aimed to equip students with skills to navigate India’s multilingual society, and overcoming linguistic barriers among different linguistic regions.

Rooted in the 1950 constitutional adoption of Hindi as the official language and Article 351’s mandate to promote it and Article 29-30’s protection of minority languages, the policy envisioned a generation fluent in three languages. It sought to balance Hindi’s national reach, English’s utility and regional languages’ cultural depth, embodying India’s constitutional ethos of ‘**Unity in Diversity**’.

---

## Controversies and Regional Resistance

The three-language formula (1968) triggered fierce opposition, exposing India’s regional fissures. Tamil Nadu’s DMK-led protests branded it as imposition of Hindi, followed by demonstrations—including self-immolations—which escalated post-1968. Critics argued it violated federalism, which assigns education and culture to the states. The 1956 States Reorganisation Commission had reinforced linguistic identities by redrawing state boundaries based on language, empowering regional languages.

Several southern states like Tamil Nadu resented imposition of Hindi language. The linguistic diversity in northeast adds another layer of complexity. In Nagaland, with over 16 dialects like Angami and Sema, choosing one ‘regional language’ risked exclusion of other dialects spoken in state. Manipur’s Meitei-Kuki divide echoed this challenge.

## Three-Language Formula: Benefits and Challenges

**Benefits**:
- Exposure to diverse languages fosters cultural exchanges and empathy.
- Hindi aids intra-national mobility, while English unlocks global opportunities in trade and technology.
- Regional languages preserve cultural identity, ensuring cultural roots endure the waves of modernization.

**Challenges**:
- Resource shortages—few teachers for languages like Bhojpuri or Kokborok—plague rural schools, while urban centers fare better.
- The policy’s rigidity ignores local contexts. In Tripura, with Bengali and Kokborok coexisting, a third language disrupts education.
- Compulsion breeds resentment—not choice.
- Urban-rural gap exacerbates this: city students access digital tools, while rural classrooms lack basics, highlighting socio-economic barriers to success.

---

## Contemporary Context and Policy Evolution

The **National Education Policy (NEP) 2020** reimagines the three-language formula, offering flexibility. States can choose any three languages—regional, classical, or national—as per local needs. Andhra Pradesh might select Telugu, English, and Tamil, while Rajasthan may opt for Hindi, English, and Gujarati. This addresses Hindi-centric criticism. The NEP’s mother-tongue focus up to Grade 5, backed by retention studies, enhances inclusivity.

Internationally, Switzerland, Singapore, and Canada show multilingualism can be successful. In similar fashion, India can devolve choices to states, fostering voluntary multilingualism nationally.

---

## Conclusion

The Three-Language Formula reflects India’s vision of strengthening unity amidst cultural diversity, including linguistic diversity. However, its rigid past sowed discord. Its merits—cultural exchange, economic edge, and cohesion—are offset by resource gaps, regional resistance and disparities. The NEP 2020’s flexibility aligns with cooperative federalism, but regional complexities and rural-urban divides still persist.

A forward-looking policy must prioritize choice and equity. The Centre should shift to incentives—scholarships, exchanges and AI-driven platforms—over compulsion. Teacher training must bridge rural-urban gaps, ensuring quality education.

## Interview-Style Questions & Sample Answers

**Q1. What is the Three-Language Formula and why was it introduced?**  
**Answer:** Sir/Ma’am, the Three-Language Formula, introduced in 1968 under the National Policy on Education, recommended that students learn three languages: the regional/mother tongue, Hindi (as the official language), and English (or another modern language). It was introduced to promote national integration, facilitate communication across regions, and bridge the linguistic divide between the Hindi-speaking north and the diverse south and east.

**Q2. What were the major controversies surrounding the Three-Language Formula?**  
**Answer:** The policy faced strong resistance, especially in Tamil Nadu, where it was perceived as Hindi imposition. This led to protests and self-immolations. Critics argued it undermined federalism and linguistic identities reinforced by the 1956 States Reorganisation. Regional aspirations and fears of cultural dominance were the core issues.

**Q3. How has NEP 2020 modified the approach to the Three-Language Formula?**  
**Answer:** NEP 2020 introduces flexibility, allowing states to choose any three languages based on local needs instead of a rigid formula. It emphasizes mother-tongue education up to Grade 5 and promotes multilingualism without compulsion, aligning with cooperative federalism and addressing past criticisms.

**Q4. Can linguistic unity override regional sovereignty?**  
**Answer:** While linguistic unity strengthens national integration, it should not override regional sovereignty. India’s strength lies in ‘Unity in Diversity’. A balanced approach through incentives, choice, and respect for regional languages, as envisaged in NEP 2020, is more sustainable than imposition.

**Q5. What are the benefits of multilingualism in the Indian context?**  
**Answer:** Multilingualism fosters cultural exchange, enhances cognitive abilities, improves employability, and promotes national integration. Hindi aids internal mobility, English provides global access, and regional languages preserve cultural heritage.

**Q6. What challenges remain in implementing language policy in India?**  
**Answer:** Key challenges include teacher shortages for regional languages, urban-rural digital and educational divides, resource constraints in rural areas, and balancing national unity with regional aspirations. Addressing these requires investment in teacher training and technology.

**Q7. How can India balance national unity with linguistic diversity in the 21st century?**  
**Answer:** By adopting a flexible, incentive-based approach under NEP 2020, promoting voluntary multilingualism, leveraging technology (AI translation), investing in teacher training, and respecting federal principles. Learning from successful models like Switzerland and Singapore can help India turn linguistic diversity into a strength.
