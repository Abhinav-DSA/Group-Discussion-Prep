## Introduction

Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) are fundamental principles essential for achieving success in the 21st century. **Gender inclusion** is a crucial driver of building **social capital** for achieving economic growth that goes beyond diversity.

Gender inclusion is about creating an environment where people of all genders feel valued, respected, and empowered to contribute their unique perspectives and talents that builds social capital. **Social capital** refers to the networks of social relations, trust norms and civic participation that facilitate cooperation and mutual benefit within a society or group.

However, barriers to gender inclusion across various domains, including education, the workplace, healthcare, and societal norms are creating obstacles in building social capital.

---

## How Gender Inclusion Builds Social Capital

Gender inclusion builds social capital by fostering diverse perspectives, enhancing trust and promoting equity within communities.

- **Reduce inequalities**: Gender inclusion reduces systemic barriers that prevent equal access to opportunities. For example, the percentage of women in Kosovo Police has risen to 15 per cent since the establishment of the Association of Women in the Kosovo Police.

- **Social development**: Gender-balanced engagement leads to more equitable policies, better resource allocation and stronger social cohesion. Self-Help Groups in India positively impact social development, particularly for women, by empowering them financially, promoting community participation and fostering social capital.

- **Bridge gender digital divide**: Digital literacy is crucial for maintaining and expanding relationships with family, friends, and the community, positively influencing the formation of social capital. For example, Mahila Samakhya Yojana (1989) had improved social interactions among women across caste and religious boundaries through digital literacy.

- **Economic opportunities**: Gender equality and equal rights are economically important, potentially increasing GDP in emerging markets by almost 8% by increasing the female labour force participation by 5-9%, as per IMF report. Women entrepreneurs accounts grew from 55,644 to 1,90,844 under Stand-Up India scheme between November 2018 to November 2024.

- **Leadership representation**: Encouraging diverse leadership builds more inclusive institutions and decision-making bodies. For example, Companies like General Motors (GM) and Accenture have strong female representation, with GM having a Board of Director composed of 7 women out of 13 members. In Accenture, 50% of Board of Directors and 27% of global management committee are women.

## Different Barriers Present in Achieving Gender Inclusion

Women face occupation segregation in both private and public spheres, compounded by multiple barriers including:

- **Cultural norms and stereotypes**: Deeply rooted patriarchal norms and gender stereotypes perpetuate inequality by assigning specific roles to men and women. For instance, women frequently face societal expectations to focus on domestic roles rather than career ambitions, which restricts their involvement in the workforce.

- **Lack of education and awareness**: Structural barriers, and cultural biases prevent girls from accessing quality education or pursuing certain fields of study. In 2011, the female literacy rate was 64.63%, compared to 80.88% for men, creating a gap of 17.2 percentage points in India.

- **Lack of access to resources**: Women often face restricted access to financial services, property ownership and digital freedom. According to CGIAR, gender disparities in food insecurity have increased significantly, rising from 1.7% in 2019 to over 4% in 2021. Further, an estimated 73.5 per cent of women in wage employment worldwide lack access to social protection.

- **Limited representation**: Women are significantly underrepresented in political, academic and leadership positions, which contributes to ongoing systemic inequalities. As per WEF’s Global Gender Gap Report 2024, the gender gaps remain the largest in Political Empowerment (77.5% unaddressed) and Economic Participation and Opportunity (39.5% unaddressed).

- **Violence and harassment**: Gender-Based Violence (GBV) such as domestic violence at homes, harassment at workplaces and public spaces, leading to significant physical and emotional harm to women and limiting their mobility and participation in public life. The National Family Health Survey (NFHS) 5 reveals that 31.9% married women aged 18 to 49 experience domestic violence.

## Measures to Break Down Barriers for Gender Inclusion

- **Gender-responsive governance**: Through integration of gender perspectives into policy formulation, budgeting and service delivery.

- **Political empowerment**: By ensuring effective implementation of women’s reservation in local bodies and enforcement of Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam (2023) in Legislative Assemblies and the Lok Sabha.

- **Strict implementation for equal pay**: For equal work through Code on Wages, 2019 which subsumes the Equal Remuneration Act, 1976, among other acts.

- **Leadership development for women and marginalized genders**: By providing scholarships and fellowships as well as institutional training to them.

- **Accelerate equal opportunities for skilling in technology**: By promoting digital literacy and access to ICT tools, and by making gender-sensitive curricula in STEM education.

## Initiatives to Achieve Gender Inclusion

### Global Initiatives

- **He For She**: It is United Nations solidarity movement that invites people of all genders to stand in solidarity with women to create a bold, visible and united force for gender equality.

- **Gender Equality Accelerators**: The ten Gender Equality Accelerators offers solutions to achieve women and girls’ human rights and SDG 5 by providing a flexible framework for driving progress instead of ‘one size fits all’ solution.

- **Sustainable Development Goal 5**: It aims to achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls through elimination of discrimination, forced marriages and genital mutilation and ensuring full participation in leadership and decision making.

- **World Bank Group Gender Strategy 2024-30**: It puts forward the bold ambition to accelerate gender equality for a sustainable, resilient, and inclusive future in alignment with the World Bank Evolution Roadmap.

### National Initiatives

- **Preference to women in government scheme**: Government has launched women focused scheme such as Mahila Samriddhi Yojana (MSY) for microfinance, Beti Bachao Beti Padhao (BBBP) to address child sex ratio decline, Lakhpati Didi Scheme for sustainable livelihood practices etc.

- **Affirmative action to women**: The 73rd and 74th Constitutional Amendment Acts provides reservation of one-third of seats for women in panchayats and municipalities respectively, and the Constitution (106th Amendment) Act, 2023 seeks to reserve one-third of the total number of seats in Lok Sabha and state legislative assemblies for women.

### State Initiatives

- **Reservation in Government Jobs**: Women have been given 35% reservation in government jobs in the state of Bihar.

- **Jyotiba Phule Shramik Kanyadan Yojana**: Uttar Pradesh government provides financial assistance for the marriage of daughters of registered workers in state.

- **Ladli Behna Yojana**: Madhya Pradesh’s Mukhya Mantri Ladli Behna Yojana provides financial support to women, and the Ladli Laxmi Yojana, designed to enhance the health and education of girls. Similar schemes have also been introduced in other states such as Odisha (Subhadra Yojana), West Bengal (Lakshmi Bhandar Yojana), etc.

## Conclusion

Gender inclusion fosters fresh perspectives, innovative thinking, and the willingness to challenge the status quo. When all genders participate equally in social, economic, and political spheres, it broadens networks and enables the exchange of diverse views. This not only reduces discrimination but also builds mutual respect and social cohesion, thereby strengthening the foundation of social capital.

## Interview-Style Questions & Sample Answers

**Q1. What is social capital and how does gender inclusion help in building it?**  
**Answer:** Sir/Ma’am, Social capital refers to the networks of social relations, trust norms, and civic participation that facilitate cooperation and mutual benefit within a society. Gender inclusion builds it by fostering diverse perspectives, reducing inequalities, promoting equitable policies, and enhancing community cohesion. Initiatives like Self-Help Groups in India have empowered women financially while strengthening social bonds across caste and religious lines.

**Q2. What are the major barriers to gender inclusion in India?**  
**Answer:** Major barriers include deeply rooted patriarchal cultural norms and stereotypes, lack of education and awareness (female literacy gap of ~17%), restricted access to resources, limited political and leadership representation, and high prevalence of gender-based violence (NFHS-5 data shows ~32% of married women face domestic violence). These create systemic obstacles to women’s full participation.

**Q3. How do schemes like Beti Bachao Beti Padhao and Lakhpati Didi contribute to gender inclusion?**  
**Answer:** Beti Bachao Beti Padhao focuses on improving child sex ratio and girl child education, while Lakhpati Didi Scheme promotes women’s entrepreneurship and sustainable livelihoods. Combined with 33% women’s reservation in Panchayats and the Nari Shakti Vandan Adhiniyam 2023, these initiatives enhance political, economic, and social empowerment.

**Q4. Compare global frameworks with India’s approach to gender inclusion.**  
**Answer:** Global frameworks like UN HeForShe, SDG 5, and World Bank Gender Strategy 2024-30 provide broad guidelines. India has localized these through constitutional amendments, women-centric schemes (BBBP, MSY), and state-level initiatives. India’s unique strength lies in large-scale SHG movements, though challenges in implementation and cultural change persist.

**Q5. What measures would you suggest to overcome barriers to gender inclusion?**  
**Answer:** I would suggest gender-responsive governance, strict enforcement of equal pay laws, leadership training and scholarships for women, STEM education with gender-sensitive curricula, widespread digital literacy programs, and robust implementation of reservation policies. Continuous monitoring and public awareness campaigns are equally important.

**Q6. How does gender inclusion impact economic growth?**  
**Answer:** Greater gender inclusion can boost GDP significantly. As per IMF estimates, increasing female labour force participation can raise GDP by nearly 8% in emerging markets. Diverse leadership also leads to better decision-making, as seen in companies like GM and Accenture, fostering innovation and inclusive growth.

**Q7. Is women’s reservation in legislatures sufficient for achieving gender equality?**  
**Answer:** It is a crucial step but not sufficient by itself. Reservation must be supported by education, skill development, safety measures, economic opportunities, and societal mindset change. Sustained multi-sectoral efforts are needed to translate representation into meaningful empowerment and social capital building.
